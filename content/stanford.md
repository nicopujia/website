### Short questions (1–50 words)

1. **What is the most significant challenge that society faces today?**

    The lack of critical thinking and misinformation isn't just a problem by itself: without solving it, we won't solve the rest of the problems either. It's a meta-problem, the root cause.

2. **How did you spend your last two summers?**

    Built personal projects, including an online, real-time multiplayer video game; taught myself data structures and algorithms; went on holidays with my family, reading Steve Jobs’ biography, Harari, and Djokovic; played soccer at my club; did calisthenics and went running with friends.

3. **What historical moment or event do you wish you could have witnessed?**

    January 1895: audiences watching the Lumière brothers' train film and fleeing the theater, believing it would hit them. I want to witness the moment technology creates wonder indistinguishable from magic. That's what I want to build someday: innovations so surprising that people can't process them with existing mental models.

4. **Briefly elaborate on one of your extracurricular activities, a job you hold, or responsibilities you have for your family.**

    At 16, I built my first commercial project (Siderplast). I worked three months without knowing if I'd be paid. When they finally asked my rate, they paid me 10% more than I requested. With those earnings, I bought my first fully owned computer.

5. **List five things that are important to you.**

    Building useful things; improving on what I decide to focus on; not having to worry about money or
    being limited by my physique; spending time with people I care about; and exploring, learning, and
    understanding the world.

### Short Essays (100–250 words)

#### Excitement about learning

> The Stanford community is deeply curious and driven to learn in and out of the classroom. Reflect on an idea or experience that makes you genuinely excited about learning.

I was browsing YouTube after finishing a programming course, when a video titled "How does the Internet work?" caught my attention. My mom reminded me to do my geography homework, but I watched the video instead.

Then I started wondering: how do computers work inside? I found explanations of the binary system, researched how it runs on hardware, and discovered the book "But How Do It Know?" My mom kept insisting on the homework, but with this book in my hands, her words went in one ear and out the other.

I read most of the book, learning how CPU, RAM, and I/O work. Then came the revelation: you can add numbers, run programs, do anything a computer does, just with complex arrangements of NAND gates. I could finally trace the complete path from keystroke to transistors to screen, and stopped reading once I could mentally model the remaining topics.

Immediately, I wanted to apply the networking knowledge, so I built an online multiplayer game. That led me deeper into protocols and communication layers. It's like a tree: every branch of knowledge reveals nested ones beneath it.

I think this will never finish. Most recently, I spent two weeks learning a text editor's keyboard shortcuts and customizing every configuration file. I had to force myself to stop because the time spent configuring outweighed the productivity gains.

I can't stand gaps in my mental model of things I'm curious about. Geography homework can wait. Understanding how things actually work cannot.

#### Note to future roommate

> Virtually all of Stanford's undergraduates live on campus. Write a note to your future roommate that reveals something about you or that will help your roommate – and us – get to know you better.

Hey,

You'll probably find me playing soccer every once in a while with friends. For me, it's one of the few times I'm completely present, not thinking about work or what I’ll do the following days, just what's happening on the pitch. I'm still grateful every time I can run as much as I want. I have this memory stuck in my head of getting exhausted after 3 minutes in a match, before I discovered I had celiac disease. So yeah, I don't take it for granted.

Speaking of celiac: fair warning, I can't eat normal bread, pizza, or pasta. Well, I *could*, but the disease just massively increases the punishment for doing so. It's annoying, sure, but the forced discipline led me to further improve my health way beyond just fixing the gluten problem, so it’s a good trade, I think.

At night, I often read 30 minutes or so before sleep. Right now it's *The Righteous Mind*, which argues that different cultures have genuinely different moral foundations, not just different applications of the same values. Makes me think about how many of my own assumptions I've just absorbed without questioning. Happy to dive into it if you're interested.

Anyway, looking forward to meeting you in person. And teaching you about *mate* and *truco*.

See you soon,

Nico

#### Distinctive contribution

> Please describe what aspects of your life experiences, interests, and character would help you make a distinctive contribution as an undergraduate to Stanford University.

At 15, I finished Game Finder and wanted to publish it. Google Play Store costs $25 one-time; Apple App Store requires $100 per year, plus a MacBook. Being in Argentina, even $25 was significant. I asked my grandma to gift it to me.

But to build that, I shared the home PC with my brother, negotiating time or waiting. Running certain tools consumed 100% CPU and crashed the system, limiting what I could attempt. To earn money, receiving international payments meant abusive taxes, regulations, and consultants. Too complex to burden my parents with for personal projects, so I was limited to local opportunities paid in cash.

Eventually, I accumulated $2,620 and spent it all on a $2,000 MacBook and $620 iPhone, betting they would multiply what I could build. Down to zero, trusting I'd earn more.

They did. The MacBook didn't crash in my first hackathon, and the iPhone let me test the app properly. That investment taught me where resources should go: invest in what compounds your capability, and cut everything else.

At Stanford, when teams hit compute budgets on LLM research projects, I won't assume we need more money. I've built systems on free hosting because I had to, so I’ll contribute to making limited resources work. And when designing products, I'll push us to consider users beyond Silicon Valley conditions: people on shared devices, undervalued currencies, and complex payment barriers, because I was that user.
